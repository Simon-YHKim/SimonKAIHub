## Recent X/consumer signals on privacy/consent toggle language and UX in personal data / self-knowledge / reflection apps (KR-relevant)

- Privacy and consent toggles are one of the highest-stakes trust moments. Default-on or vague language ("enable smart features", "improve experience") is repeatedly called out as extractive or creepy, especially in KR communities with higher baseline privacy sensitivity. Clear, reversible, benefit-first framing ("your entries stay private unless you choose to share patterns with tomorrow's you") significantly increases comfort and repeated deep engagement.
- Shared components (SwitchRow, PreferenceToggle) for privacy/consent/formats are an opportunity to make the *entire* app feel consistent and respectful. Inconsistent or technical toggle copy breaks the "this is my private space" feeling built by the recent plain-language wins (capture, jarvis citations, manual onboarding, assessment feedback toasts).
- KR users show stronger negative reaction to anything that feels like data is leaving the device without explicit, granular control. Explicit "you decide what syncs or exports" language outperforms feature lists and directly supports the local-first / anti-lock-in positioning that has performed well in prior GTM-related signals.
- Formats and export toggles (how personal reflection data can leave the device) are particularly sensitive. Users in personal knowledge spaces want explicit control and plain explanations at the point of decision. This aligns with the "AI는 도구, 주체는 당신" and "no company can take this away from you" framing that has been directionally validated.
- The recent humanizing copy work (jargon removal, friendly titles, calm toast feedback) has already moved the interface toward "respects me as the subject." Clean, consistent privacy/consent shared components will compound that — when the toggles themselves speak in the same plain, user-first voice and the native implementation is solid (AG lane), the whole experience reinforces ownership and reduces the "creepy" perception that kills long-term use in self-knowledge tools.
