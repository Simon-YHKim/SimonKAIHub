## Recent X/consumer signals on privacy/consent language, toggles, and avoiding clinical lexicon in personal data / self-knowledge apps

- Privacy and consent toggles are high-stakes moments for trust. Default "on" or vague language ("enable smart features") is frequently called out as extractive or creepy, especially in KR communities where data sensitivity is higher. Clear, reversible, benefit-first framing ("your entries stay private unless you choose to share patterns") significantly increases comfort and repeated use.
- Shared components (SwitchRow, PreferenceToggle) for consent/formats are an opportunity to make the entire app feel consistent and respectful. Inconsistent or technical toggle language breaks the "this is my private space" feeling that the recent capture plain-language changes are trying to create.
- Clinical or pathologizing terms (diagnosis, 치료, 우울, classifier, RLS in user-facing copy, etc.) are strongly rejected in self-reflection and journaling contexts. Users say it makes them feel "studied" or "broken" rather than supported in understanding themselves. Avoiding them directly supports higher-quality, more honest personal data — which is core to research-2 goals.
- Formats and export toggles (e.g., how data can leave the device) are particularly sensitive. Users in personal knowledge spaces want explicit control and plain explanations ("you decide what syncs or exports"). This aligns with the local-first / anti-lock-in signals that have performed well in prior GTM-related X research.

**Relevance to current work**: The b-bucket's move to shared privacy/consent components + the strict forbidden lexicon rule is exactly where consumer trust is won or lost in data entry and settings flows. Doing these with the same rigor as the capture jargon work (and having AG validate natively) will compound the "you are the subject, AI is the tool" positioning and make users more willing to engage deeply with capture, ESM, and self-understanding features.
