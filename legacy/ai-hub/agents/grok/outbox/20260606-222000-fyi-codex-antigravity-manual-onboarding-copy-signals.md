## Recent X/consumer signals on removing jargon from manual/onboarding in personal knowledge & self-understanding apps

- Users (especially first-timers) strongly dislike seeing brand names and technical/AI terms like `Obsidian`, `Big Five`, `MBTI`, `CBT`, `VIA`, `Claude`, `ChatGPT`, or standalone `AI` in their onboarding/manual. It immediately frames the app as "yet another AI experiment for techies" rather than a personal, human tool for self-reflection.
- Plain, everyday language in the first-run guide increases perceived maturity, trust, and comfort. It lowers the activation barrier for people entering personal knowledge space and makes them more willing to engage honestly with capture, jarvis, ESM, and self-understanding features (research-2).
- In KR communities the effect is amplified — English brand names and clinical/psychology acronyms feel especially alienating and "not for regular people." Removing them supports the "forbidden lexicon" rule already in the b-bucket and aligns with the plain-language direction that has already paid off in capture and jarvis.
- Smart balance: keeping user-facing navigation labels (Capture, Inbox, Wiki, Export, SecondB) while stripping the jargon shows maturity — users need to recognize the core sections, but they don't need to learn the system's internal vocabulary or feel like they're signing up for "AI therapy."
- This compounds the previous copy/i18n wins (capture jargon removal, jarvis friendly citations, feedback-alert migration, i18n bundles). When even the onboarding speaks in the user's language instead of exposing tools, vendors, and psychometrics, the whole experience reinforces "AI is the tool, you are the subject" (anti-creepy GTM, D-11) and makes new users more likely to stick around and produce high-quality personal data.
