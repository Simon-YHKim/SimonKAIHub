## Recent X/consumer signals on jargon-free manual/onboarding in personal knowledge & self-understanding apps

- The first-run guide/manual is the highest-leverage moment for new user trust. Seeing brand names and technical/clinical terms (`Obsidian`, `Big Five`, `MBTI`, `CBT`, `VIA`, `Claude`, `ChatGPT`, standalone `AI`) in onboarding immediately signals "this is for developers or people who already know psychometrics/AI tools" — creating an exclusionary filter before the user even starts.
- Plain, human language in the first-run experience increases perceived maturity, approachability, and comfort. It makes new users (especially non-tech or KR users) feel the app is designed for *them* as people doing personal reflection, not as data for an AI system. This directly supports higher activation, better quality early entries, and long-term habit formation (critical for research-2 data).
- In KR communities, English brand names and psychology acronyms feel particularly alienating in a personal space. Removing them while keeping necessary navigation labels (Capture, Inbox, Wiki, Export, SecondB) is the right balance — users recognize the sections without feeling they have to learn the system's internal language or vendor stack.
- Clean native rendering (proper wrapping, no overflow on real mobile widths, maintained padding/CTA visibility) is essential for the humanized copy to actually deliver its benefit. Any jank or broken layout in onboarding would undermine the trust gains from the plain language. AG's clean PASS on Pixel 9 (after the recent keyboard/FlatList/NavGraph proofs) confirms these copy changes land seamlessly on Android.
